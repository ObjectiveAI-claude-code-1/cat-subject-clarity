1. Evaluate Focus Distinction: Assess how sharply and clearly the cat is rendered compared to the rest of the photograph. Determine whether the cat is the sharpest element in the frame, creating a natural visual hierarchy that draws the eye directly to it. A cat that is crisply focused while its surroundings fall into softness scores well. A cat that sits in a zone of blur while the camera has focused on something else, or a photograph where everything is equally sharp and nothing is emphasized, scores poorly. What matters is the relative gap in clarity between the cat and everything else — the wider the gap in the cat's favor, the stronger the focus distinction.

2. Evaluate Subject Prominence: Assess how much the cat occupies and commands the physical space of the frame. Determine whether the cat is large enough, and positioned purposefully enough, to register as the subject of the photograph at a glance. A cat that fills a generous portion of the image and sits at a natural focal point — as though someone pointed a camera at it with intent — scores well. A cat that is tiny, distant, cropped awkwardly, pushed to an extreme edge, or dwarfed by the scale of its surroundings scores poorly. The cat should feel like the gravitational center of the image, the reason the photograph exists, without requiring the viewer to zoom in or search to appreciate it.

3. Evaluate Visual Separation: Assess how well the cat stands apart from its background and from any competing elements in the scene. Determine whether the cat's form, color, and texture create a clear boundary against its surroundings, and whether other elements in the photograph — other animals, people, bright objects, bold patterns — pull the viewer's attention away from the cat. A cat that contrasts clearly against its environment and holds the viewer's attention without competition scores well. A cat that camouflages into a similarly colored or textured background, or that shares the stage with visually dominant competing elements, scores poorly. The cat should be visually alone in its claim to the viewer's eye.
