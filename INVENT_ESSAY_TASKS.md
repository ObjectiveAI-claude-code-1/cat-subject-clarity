Task 1 — Evaluate Focus Distinction: Examine the photograph and judge how sharply the cat is rendered compared to everything else in the image. The cat should appear more clearly focused than its surroundings — standing out as the crispest, most defined element in the scene. A cat that is sharply in focus while the background falls into softness or blur scores highly, because the eye is naturally drawn to the sharpest point in any image. A cat that is soft or out of focus while other parts of the image are sharp scores poorly, because the photograph is directing attention away from the cat. This evaluation is not about whether the image is technically sharp overall — it is about whether the cat enjoys a focus advantage over its environment. An image where everything is equally sharp offers no focus-based guidance and scores lower than one where the cat alone is crisp. What matters is the gap in clarity between the cat and the rest of the frame: the wider that gap in the cat's favor, the stronger the focus distinction.

Task 2 — Evaluate Subject Prominence: Examine the photograph and judge how much visual weight the cat carries within the frame. Consider the cat's scale relative to the overall image — how much of the composition it occupies — and whether its placement within the frame gives it natural command over the viewer's attention. A cat that fills the frame or dominates the composition is impossible to overlook and scores highly. A cat that is small, marginal, or tucked into a corner of a much larger scene scores poorly, because the viewer must search the image to locate it rather than having it register immediately. Prominence is primarily about size and position. A close-up portrait of a cat's face has maximum prominence. A wide landscape where the cat is a distant speck has almost none. However, a cat does not need to fill the entire frame to score well — a modestly sized cat placed at the compositional center of attention, where the geometry of the image naturally guides the eye, can still carry strong visual weight. The question is whether the cat reads as the subject at a glance, or whether the frame overwhelms it.

Task 3 — Evaluate Visual Isolation: Examine the photograph and judge how cleanly the cat separates from its surroundings and from any other elements competing for the viewer's attention. This task has two dimensions. First, assess background separation: does the cat's color, texture, and form contrast against what is immediately behind and around it, or does the cat blend into and camouflage against a similarly colored or textured background? A white cat on a dark floor is well-separated; a gray cat on a gray couch is not. Second, assess freedom from competition: are there other visually demanding elements in the scene — other animals, people, brightly colored objects, bold patterns, text, or any other eye-catching presences — that fight the cat for the viewer's attention? A cat alone on a clean, contrasting surface with no distractions is fully isolated and scores highly. A cat lost among busy surroundings, competing subjects, and visual clutter scores poorly. Visual isolation ensures that the viewer's eye not only finds the cat but stays on the cat, with nothing else in the image pulling attention away.
