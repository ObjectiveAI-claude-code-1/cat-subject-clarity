Task 1 – Evaluate Focus Clarity: Assess how sharply and crisply the cat is rendered relative to the rest of the photograph. The cat should be the sharpest element in the frame — the thing the viewer's eye is drawn to through detail and definition. This is not a measure of the image's absolute resolution or technical quality; it is a measure of relative focus. A cat whose fur texture, facial features, and body edges are rendered with more precision and clarity than its surroundings demonstrates strong focus clarity. A cat that appears soft, blurred, or indistinct while other elements in the image are sharp demonstrates weak focus clarity. The task is to determine whether the focus of the photograph belongs to the cat, making it the perceptual anchor of the image.

Task 2 – Evaluate Subject Prominence: Assess how much visual real estate and commanding presence the cat holds within the frame. The cat should feel like the reason the photograph was taken — not a small detail within a larger scene. This task considers the cat's size relative to the overall image, its positioning within the frame, and its visual weight against the surrounding environment. A cat that occupies a generous portion of the image and is placed with intention has high subject prominence. A cat that is tiny, tucked into a corner, or dwarfed by an expansive background has low subject prominence. The task must also account for context: a cat against a simple, uncluttered background can feel prominent without filling the entire frame, while a cat in a busy, complex environment needs greater size and positioning to avoid being swallowed by visual noise.

Task 3 – Evaluate Visual Separation: Assess how distinctly and clearly the cat stands apart from its background and from any competing visual elements in the photograph. This task operates on two levels. First, determine whether the cat is visually distinguishable from its immediate surroundings — whether the cat's form is easy to perceive at a glance, or whether it blends, camouflages, or merges into the environment through similar colors, textures, or patterns. A black cat on a black surface or a white cat against snow are examples of weak background separation. Second, determine whether the cat competes with other visually arresting elements in the frame — other animals, people, bold objects, or dramatic scenery that could pull the viewer's attention away from the cat. Strong visual separation means the cat is the singular, undeniable focal point, and nothing else in the image challenges its dominance.
