1. Evaluate the focus dominance of the cat in the photograph. Assess whether the cat is the sharpest and most crisply rendered element in the image relative to its surroundings. A cat that is in clear focus while the background falls away into softness draws the viewer's eye naturally and effortlessly. A cat that is blurry, soft, or no sharper than everything else in the frame loses its focal authority. This task is not concerned with absolute image sharpness or technical resolution — it measures whether the cat holds a focus advantage over the rest of the scene, such that the optics of the photograph direct the viewer's attention toward the cat as the thing the camera was trying to see.

2. Evaluate the subject prominence of the cat in the photograph. Assess whether the cat occupies enough of the frame, with sufficient size and intentional placement, to register immediately as the subject. A cat that fills a commanding portion of the image and sits in a natural compositional position reads as the reason the photograph was taken. A cat that is tiny, pushed to the margins, awkwardly clipped at the edges, or dwarfed by the scale of its environment reads as incidental — something the photographer happened to include rather than intended to capture. This task measures whether the cat has been given enough visual real estate and positional weight to be perceived not merely as a thing in the image, but as the thing in the image.

3. Evaluate the visual separation of the cat from its surroundings in the photograph. Assess whether the cat is visually distinct from the background and free from competing elements that pull the viewer's attention away. A cat that blends into similarly colored or patterned surfaces — a gray cat on a gray couch, a tabby on a mottled rug, a black cat in a dark room — loses its identity as a distinct subject and becomes part of the scene's texture. Likewise, a photograph crowded with other animals, people, bright objects, or bold patterns forces the cat to compete for the viewer's gaze. This task measures whether the cat exists on its own visual plane, standing apart from both the background and any other elements in the scene through sufficient contrast in color, tone, texture, or spatial boundaries.
