1. Evaluate the focus distinction of the cat in the photograph. Judge whether the cat appears crisp and defined relative to the rest of the image. A cat that is in sharp focus while its surroundings fall into softness or blur creates a visual hierarchy that draws the eye directly to it. A cat that is equally sharp as — or softer than — the objects around it flattens that hierarchy and becomes just another element in the scene. Also consider whether the cat's own features — its eyes, fur texture, the edges of its ears and body — are rendered with enough internal clarity that the viewer can immediately recognize the subject as a cat, rather than perceiving only motion, color, or an indistinct shape. This task is not about measuring absolute technical sharpness or pixel-level resolution. It is about whether the focus of the photograph serves the cat by guiding the viewer's eye toward it and away from everything else.

2. Evaluate the frame presence of the cat in the photograph. Judge whether the cat occupies enough of the image to register as the dominant visual element rather than an incidental detail within a larger scene. A cat that fills a generous portion of the frame announces itself immediately. A cat that occupies only a tiny fraction of the image — a small shape in a vast room, a distant figure in a landscape — does not command attention regardless of how sharp or well-lit it may be. The cat does not need to fill the entire frame, but it must be large enough to be perceived as the main event at a glance. Also consider the visual weight of the cat relative to its surroundings: a cat at moderate size against a plain, uncluttered background can still command the image because nothing else competes for spatial attention, while a cat of the same size within a visually dense or busy environment may feel diminished because the surrounding detail dilutes its presence.

3. Evaluate the visual isolation of the cat in the photograph. Judge whether the cat is visually separable from its background and from any other elements in the frame that might compete for the viewer's attention. A cat that contrasts against its surroundings — by color, tone, or brightness — is easy to perceive as a distinct subject. A cat that blends into a similarly colored or textured background loses its visual identity and becomes harder to distinguish from the scene. Also consider whether competing elements are present in the image: other animals, people, or prominent objects that could plausibly be read as the subject of the photograph. The presence of such elements divides the viewer's attention and weakens the cat's claim as the clear subject. This task does not require the cat to be the only thing in the photograph. It requires the cat to be visually distinct — standing apart from the background and not overshadowed by anything else in the frame.
