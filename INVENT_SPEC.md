Evaluate the subject clarity of the cat in the photograph. Score how well the cat serves as the clear, immediate, and undeniable subject of the image. The cat should be in focus, visually distinct from its surroundings, and instantly recognizable as the center of the photograph at first glance — without requiring the viewer to search, squint, or zoom in.

Consider the following factors when scoring:

- Is the cat in focus and sharply rendered relative to the rest of the image?
- Is the cat large enough in the frame to register as the subject, or is it too small to command attention?
- Does the cat contrast sufficiently against its surroundings, or does it blend into a similarly colored or textured background?
- Are there competing visual elements — other animals, people, objects, or patterns — that pull the viewer's eye away from the cat?
- When glancing at the photograph for a fraction of a second, is the cat the first thing the viewer notices?

A photograph with strong subject clarity makes the cat plainly, unmistakably present. The cat commands the image. A photograph with weak subject clarity forces the viewer to work to find or identify the cat — it may be out of focus, too small, camouflaged against the background, or visually overshadowed by competing elements.

Technical resolution and sharpness are secondary to perceptual clarity. A slightly soft image where the cat is still clearly the dominant visual element should score higher than a razor-sharp image where the cat is lost among distractions. This evaluation is about whether the cat owns the photograph.

When inventing sub-functions for this task, each sub-function should evaluate a specific dimension of subject clarity — such as focus quality, subject prominence within the frame, or visual separation from competing elements. Each sub-function receives the same cat photograph as input.
